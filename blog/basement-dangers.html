5 Things in Your Basement Right Now That Could Cost You $20,000

The stuff you are not thinking about is the stuff that floods your house.

March 13, 2026 | 6 min read

Nobody thinks about their basement until it is underwater. And by that point, you are not Googling "home maintenance tips" — you are Googling "emergency flood cleanup" at 2 AM while standing in three inches of water. We have been doing this for over 37 years in BC, and the pattern is always the same: something was sitting down there for years, quietly getting worse, and one day it let go.

Here are five things we see constantly — things that are probably in your basement right now — and what they actually cost when they fail. The good news: most of them are cheap to fix if you catch them first.

1

Polybutylene Pipes (The Grey Plastic Time Bomb)

If your home was built between 1978 and 1995, there is a good chance your water supply lines are polybutylene — grey flexible plastic pipes with copper crimp rings at the joints. They were cheap, easy to install, and used in hundreds of thousands of homes across North America. They also have a fatal flaw: they deteriorate from the inside out.

Chlorine in municipal water slowly breaks down the pipe walls. Micro-fractures form inside where you cannot see them. The pipe looks perfectly fine from the outside. Then one Tuesday afternoon while you are at work, it splits open and dumps water into your basement for eight hours straight.

Insurance companies in some regions will not even cover homes with poly-b still in service. Some underwriters require replacement before they will issue a policy.

Cost to replace: $4,000 - $8,000

Cost when they burst: $15,000 - $30,000+

Check today: Look at your water supply lines. Grey flexible plastic with copper crimp rings = poly-b. Call a plumber.

2

Your Water Heater (The Ticking Clock in the Corner)

This is the number one cause of residential basement flooding that nobody talks about. Most tank water heaters last 8 to 12 years. After that, you are on borrowed time. The anode rod — the sacrificial metal bar inside that protects the tank from corrosion — eventually corrodes away completely. Once it is gone, the tank itself starts rusting from the inside.

You will not get a warning. Maybe a small puddle around the base that you wipe up and forget about. That puddle is your warning. It means the tank has already started to fail. When it goes, it goes — 40 to 60 gallons of water, all at once, plus whatever the supply line feeds in until someone shuts it off.

Cost to replace proactively: $1,500 - $3,000

Cost when it fails catastrophically: $10,000 - $25,000+

Check today: Find the label on your water heater. Look for the manufacture date. If it is 10+ years old, start planning a replacement now — not later.

3

Foundation Cracks (Small Lines, Big Problems)

Water is relentless. It finds every crack, every gap, every tiny imperfection in your foundation. During spring melt or heavy rain, hydrostatic pressure pushes groundwater against your foundation walls, and it only needs the smallest opening to get through.

A hairline crack might not let in visible water, but it lets in moisture vapor. That moisture accumulates behind finished walls, inside insulation, in places with zero air circulation. Mould does not need a flood — it just needs consistent dampness and something organic to feed on. Drywall paper is a five-star meal for mould. By the time you notice a musty smell or see discolouration, colonies have been growing behind your walls for months.

Wider cracks let in actual water. And because most basement finishes — framing, insulation, drywall — are built right against the foundation wall, homeowners never see the cracks at all. The water shows up as a wet carpet, a buckled baseboard, or a stain on the wall, and by then you are tearing things apart to find the source.

Cost to seal a crack: $300 - $800

Cost of mould remediation + rebuild: $8,000 - $20,000+

Check today: If your basement is unfinished, inspect the foundation walls with a flashlight. If it is finished, look for water stains, bubbling paint, or musty smells along the base of walls.

4

Disconnected or Short Downspouts (The $20 Fix You Are Ignoring)

This one drives us crazy because it is the cheapest, easiest fix on this entire list, and almost nobody does it. If your gutter downspouts dump water within six feet of your foundation, you are basically pouring water directly into your basement. Every single rainstorm. Every spring melt. Every time.

A single downspout during a heavy rain can discharge hundreds of gallons of water right next to your foundation wall. That water saturates the soil, builds up hydrostatic pressure, and pushes through any crack, joint, or weak point it can find. Crushed or disconnected downspouts are even worse — they dump water directly against the house at ground level.

The fix is a downspout extension. They cost about $20 at any hardware store. Extend them 6 to 10 feet away from the foundation. Make sure the ground slopes away from the house, not toward it. That is it.

Cost to fix: $15 - $30 per downspout

Cost of chronic water intrusion: $5,000 - $15,000+

Check today: Walk the perimeter of your house. Where does each downspout end? If it is within six feet of the foundation or dumping onto a splash block that is not directing water away, fix it this weekend.

5

Missing Backwater Valve (When the City Sewer Backs Up Into Your House)

This is the one that makes people's stomachs turn. If your home does not have a backwater valve on the main sewer line, a city sewer backup sends raw sewage directly into your basement. Through every drain, every toilet, every floor drain. We are talking Category 3 water — the worst classification. Raw sewage, bacteria, pathogens, chemicals. Everything that goes into a city sewer system comes back up into your home.

Many BC municipalities now require backwater valves on new construction, but older homes do not have them. If your house was built before the requirement was adopted in your area, odds are you do not have one. A backwater valve is a one-way gate on your sewer line — it lets water flow out but prevents anything from flowing back in. Simple concept, massive protection.

Sewer backups are one of the most expensive and invasive cleanups we do. Everything the sewage touches — flooring, drywall, insulation, furniture, stored belongings — has to be removed and disposed of. The space has to be decontaminated, dried, and rebuilt. It is not a cleanup. It is a gut-and-rebuild job.

Cost to install a backwater valve: $2,000 - $4,000

Cost of a sewer backup cleanup: $10,000 - $25,000+

Check today: Look for a round access cover in your basement floor near where the main sewer line exits the house. No cover? You probably do not have a backwater valve. Call a plumber and ask.

The bottom line

Every single item on this list is cheaper to deal with before it fails than after. By a factor of 5 to 10 times. The poly-b replacement that costs $6,000 today prevents a $25,000 flood next year. The $20 downspout extension prevents thousands in water intrusion damage. The $3,000 backwater valve prevents a $20,000 sewage disaster.

Go check your basement. Today. Not this weekend. Today. Look at your pipes, check your water heater label, walk outside and look at your downspouts. It takes 15 minutes and it could save you $20,000.
