Your Insurance Claim Was Denied — Now What?

A denial is not the end. Here is the appeals process most homeowners never hear about — and how to use it.

7 min read  ·  March 13, 2026

You did everything right. You called your insurance company. You filed the claim. You waited. And then you got the letter: denied.

If you are reading this, you probably feel angry, confused, and maybe a little helpless. That is completely normal. But here is what most people do not know: a denial is not the final answer. There is an appeals process, and homeowners who use it win more often than you might expect.

Most people accept a denial and move on. They assume the insurance company has the last word. They do not. You have the right to challenge the decision, and there are clear steps to do it. Let us walk through them.

Why claims get denied in the first place

Before you can fight a denial, you need to understand the reason behind it. Insurance companies deny claims for a handful of common reasons, and each one requires a different response.

  • "Maintenance issue" vs. "sudden and accidental" — This is the big one. Your policy covers sudden and accidental damage. If the insurer decides the damage resulted from long-term neglect or lack of maintenance, they will call it a maintenance issue and deny the claim. A slowly leaking pipe that finally burst is a grey area they love to exploit.
  • Failure to mitigate — Your policy requires you to take reasonable steps to prevent further damage. If you waited too long to address the water, or did not take immediate action after a fire, the insurer may argue you let the damage get worse.
  • Late reporting — Most policies require prompt notification. Waiting weeks or months to file gives the insurer grounds to question the claim.
  • Policy exclusions — Certain types of damage, such as overland flooding or sewer backup, may not be covered under your base policy. Many homeowners do not realize they need separate endorsements for these until it is too late.

A denial is not the same as a coverage limitation

If the insurance company approved part of your claim but not all of it, that is not a denial. That is a coverage limitation or a partial payment. It means they agree something is covered, but they disagree on the scope or the amount. This is worth knowing because a partial payment gives you a different starting point for negotiation. You are not arguing whether the claim is valid — you are arguing about how much it should be worth. That is a much easier fight.

The 5-step process to fight a denied claim

01

Read the denial letter carefully

The reason for the denial matters more than anything else. The letter will cite specific policy language or conditions that the insurer says were not met. Write down the exact reason. If the letter is vague, call and ask for a written explanation with the specific policy clause they are relying on. You cannot build an appeal without knowing exactly what you are appealing.

02

Review your actual policy wording

Not the summary. Not the brochure your broker gave you. The full policy document. Find the section the insurer cited and read the exact language. Insurance policies are contracts, and the words matter. You may find that the clause they cited does not actually say what they claim it says. You may also find coverage provisions they overlooked. If you do not have a copy of your full policy, your insurer is legally required to provide one.

03

Gather your evidence

This is where professional documentation makes or breaks your case. Photos taken at the time of loss, moisture readings, thermal imaging reports, air quality data, the restoration company's scope of work and daily reports — all of this is evidence. If the insurer said you failed to mitigate, your documentation proves you took immediate action. If they called it a maintenance issue, your evidence can show the damage was sudden. The stronger your paper trail, the harder it is for them to hold the denial.

04

Write a formal appeal letter

Keep it professional, factual, and specific. Reference the denial letter by date and claim number. Quote the policy language they cited. Then present your evidence and explain — point by point — why the denial does not hold up under the actual policy wording. Attach supporting documents: the restoration report, photos, moisture data, any third-party assessments. Send it by registered mail or a method that gives you proof of delivery.

05

Escalate if the appeal fails

If the insurer upholds the denial after your appeal, you still have options. In British Columbia, you can file a complaint with the General Insurance OmbudService (GIO), an independent dispute resolution body that reviews insurance complaints at no cost to you. You can also hire a public adjuster — an independent claims professional who works for you, not the insurance company. They review your policy, assess the damage independently, and negotiate on your behalf. Their fee is typically a percentage of the settlement they recover.

How to prevent a denial in the first place

The best way to win a claim dispute is to never have one. And the single biggest factor in whether a claim gets approved or denied is the quality of documentation from the start.

When a certified restoration company responds to your property, they document everything from hour one: timestamped photos, moisture readings at every affected area, thermal imaging to find hidden damage, daily progress reports, and a detailed scope of work written to IICRC standards. That documentation is not just for the restoration — it is the backbone of your insurance claim.

Insurers deny claims when the evidence is thin. When they receive a complete, professionally prepared report with objective data, they have far less room to argue. The restoration company's documentation proves when the damage occurred, what caused it, what steps were taken immediately, and why the work performed was necessary. It answers every question the adjuster will ask before they ask it.
